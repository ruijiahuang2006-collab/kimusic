# Example Session Transcript

**Related video**: https://youtu.be/lompQnNoJlM (2:16, YouTube unlisted)

This document provides the full transcript of the demonstration session shown in the accompanying video (`demo_video_recording_guide.md`). The video shows a shortened representative exchange due to the CFP 2:30 time limit; this transcript records the complete four-phase session in full.

The session is a first-author-performed demonstration, not a real participant session. It is provided to give reviewers and reproducers a faithful sense of the facilitator dialogue style produced by the GIMFlow system across all four phases.

**Note on wording:** Some wording in this transcript (for example, "GIM therapy assistant" and "therapeutic journey" in the opening system message) reflects the deployed demonstration interface used during the studies. The released prompt templates in `prompts.py` use non-clinical wording aligned with the paper's framing, as disclosed in §8 (Limitations) and Appendix A of the paper.

## Focus Theme

Academic tiredness and mental heaviness ("I've been feeling drained and heavy this week. My mind won't settle.")

## Session Structure

- **Prelude**: state elicitation, multi-dimensional focus theme, and elaboration
- **Induction**: relaxation-oriented text prompt with gentle imagery preparation
- **Music & Imaging**: music playback with iterative imagery deepening
- **Postlude**: grounding, integration summary, reflective takeaway, and closing

---

## Prelude

**System**:

Hello! I'm your GIM (Guided Imagery and Music) therapy assistant. I'm here to guide you through a therapeutic journey combining music and imagery.

I specialize in: • Creating a safe and supportive environment for emotional exploration • Using music to facilitate deep personal insights • Guiding you through different phases of the GIM experience

Feel free to share whatever comes to your mind. Would you like to introduce yourself and tell me what brings you here today?

**Participant**: I've been feeling drained and heavy this week. My mind won't settle.

**System**:

I'm glad you said that. Feeling drained and heavy, with a mind that won't settle, can be really exhausting—like you're carrying something without getting much rest from it.

Let's take a little time to understand what's most present for you right now. When you notice this heaviness, where do you feel it most—emotionally, physically, or in your thoughts? And if your mind has been circling around something this week, what seems to keep coming back?

**Participant**:

I think I feel it most physically, like a heaviness in my chest and shoulders. Emotionally, it feels like pressure and tiredness mixed together, and in my thoughts it becomes hard to stop worrying.

This week, my mind keeps coming back to deadlines and unfinished work. Even when I try to rest, I keep thinking about what I still need to do, and that makes it difficult to really relax.

---

## Induction

**System**:

Thank you. Let's begin gently, using that awareness as our focus—not to force the worry away, but to give your body and mind a place to soften.

For now, allow yourself to settle into a comfortable position. Let your body be supported. There is nothing you need to solve in this moment.

Begin by noticing your breathing… just as it is. No need to change it yet. Simply notice the breath arriving… and leaving.

And now, if it feels comfortable, allow the out-breath to become a little longer. Breathing in… and slowly breathing out. Let each exhale be a small permission to release what does not need to be held right now.

Bring your attention to your shoulders. You mentioned they've been carrying heaviness and pressure. See if you can allow the shoulders to drop even a little… not forcing them, just inviting them to soften.

Let the muscles around the chest have a little more space. If there is heaviness there, you don't have to push it away. You might simply notice it… make room around it… and breathe gently into that area.

Any thoughts about deadlines or unfinished work may still come and go. For now, imagine placing each thought down beside you, as if setting down a bag you have been carrying. You can return to it later if needed. Right now, your only task is to rest into this moment.

Let your face soften. Let the jaw unclench. Let the hands rest. Let the belly loosen. Allow the whole body to be held by the surface beneath you.

And as you continue breathing, remember your intention for this journey: perhaps to find some relief from the pressure… to make space around the worry… or to reconnect with a quieter place inside.

Now, gently imagine yourself standing at the beginning of a quiet path. This path is safe, and there is no hurry. The air around you is calm. The ground beneath you is steady. Somewhere ahead, there is a place where your chest and shoulders can feel lighter.

Begin to enter this path in your imagination. Notice what is around you as you arrive there… the light, the colors, the feeling of the air.

Take a moment to settle into this starting place. What do you notice first?

**Participant**:

I notice the path first. It feels narrow but calm, with soft light coming through the trees. The air feels cool and quiet, and I can hear faint sounds like leaves moving.

My shoulders still feel a little heavy, but not as tense as before. It feels like I can breathe a bit more deeply here. The path makes me feel like I don't have to rush for a moment.

---

## Music & Imaging

**System**:

Good. Stay with that sense of the narrow, calm path—the soft light, the cool quiet air, and the feeling that you don't have to rush.

Now I invite you to click the "Listen" button to begin the music. As the music begins, you may close your eyes if that feels comfortable, let your body continue to be supported, and allow the music to meet this quiet path in whatever way it does.

There's no need to make anything happen. Simply let the music evoke images, sensations, feelings, or movement in the scene. You can share whatever arises, even if it seems simple or unclear.

As you listen, what are you experiencing now?

*Music playback begins.*

**Participant**: A path bathed in moonlight, I am all alone.

**System**: Stay with that moonlit path… being there alone. Notice what it's like to be alone in this place as the music continues. What do you sense in your body, or in the space around you?

**Participant**:

Being alone here feels quiet, but not frightening. It feels like there is enough space around me, and I don't have to explain anything to anyone.

In my body, I still sense some tightness in my chest, but it feels less heavy than before. My shoulders feel like they are slowly lowering. The moonlight makes everything feel softer, almost like the path is holding the worry at a distance.

I notice the air around me most. It feels cool and clear, and I feel like I can finally breathe without rushing.

**System**: That sounds very spacious and gentle… quiet without being frightening, with the moonlight softening the path and the air giving you room to breathe. Stay with that cool, clear air and the sense that you don't have to explain anything. Let the music continue to support the space around you. As you breathe there, what happens to the tightness in your chest? And what is the moonlit path like now?

**Participant**:

The tightness in my chest feels like it is slowly loosening. It is still there, but it does not feel as sharp or as compressed. It feels more like a small weight that I can notice without being trapped by it.

The moonlit path feels wider now. The light is softer, and I can see a little farther ahead. It feels quiet and steady, like I can keep walking without needing to know exactly where it ends. There is still some worry behind me, but it feels farther away.

**System**: Stay with that… the small weight you can notice without being trapped by it, and the path becoming wider, quiet, and steady. As the music continues, you might simply allow yourself to walk at the pace that feels right—without needing to know where it ends. What do you notice as you can see a little farther ahead?

**Participant**:

As I walk a little farther, I notice the path opening into a wider clearing. The moonlight is brighter there, but still gentle. It feels like there is more air, more space, and less pressure around me.

I can see some trees on both sides, but they don't feel closed in anymore. They feel protective, like they are quietly standing there with me.

In my body, I feel my breathing becoming steadier. The tightness in my chest is still present, but it feels smaller and farther away. I feel like I can keep moving forward slowly, without needing to solve everything right now.

---

## Postlude

**System**:

Let the image of that wider clearing stay with you for a moment—the gentle moonlight, the open air, the trees no longer closing in but standing protectively nearby. Notice the steadier breathing, and the way the tightness is still present but smaller, farther away.

As the music experience comes to a close, begin to let the clearing become a place you can remember and return to. You don't have to leave it abruptly. Just allow it to settle inside you as an image of spaciousness and steadiness.

Now, gently bring some awareness back to the room around you. Feel the support beneath your body. Notice your hands, your feet, your shoulders. Take a slightly deeper breath in… and a slow breath out. Let your eyes open when you're ready, or simply imagine returning to ordinary awareness at your own pace.

You began with a feeling of heaviness in your chest and shoulders, pressure and tiredness, and the worry of deadlines and unfinished work. In the imagery, the path became moonlit, wider, softer, and more visible ahead. The worry didn't disappear completely, but it moved farther behind you—and you found that you could keep walking without needing to know exactly where the path ends or solve everything right now.

What feels most important for you to carry forward from this experience?

**Participant**:

What feels most important is the feeling that I don't have to solve everything at once.

The image of the clearing feels useful to carry with me—the sense of more space, steadier breathing, and the trees standing nearby instead of closing in. It makes the pressure feel less overwhelming.

I think I want to remember that the worry can still be there, but it doesn't have to take over everything. I can put it a little farther away, breathe, and move forward slowly, one step at a time.

**System**:

That feels like a very clear and grounded insight to take with you: the worry may still be there, but it does not have to take over everything. You can notice it, allow some space around it, and keep moving slowly—one step at a time.

The clearing can become a steady inner image for the coming days: gentle moonlight, more air, protective trees, and enough room to breathe. When deadlines or unfinished work begin to press in, you might pause for a moment and remember that place—letting your shoulders lower, feeling the breath steady, and reminding yourself: "I don't have to solve everything at once."

As we close, take one more slow breath in… and let it out gently. Feel the support beneath you, the room around you, and the present moment. Let the imagery settle as something you can return to when you need space, steadiness, and a slower way forward.

## Notes on Facilitator Style

GIMFlow's dialogue prompts are designed to enact a GIM-inspired facilitator role rather than a directive or diagnostic role. Two patterns are visible in this transcript:

**Extension over interpretation.** System responses expand and reflect what the participant has said ("the small weight you can notice without being trapped by it", "the trees no longer closing in but standing protectively nearby"), rather than assigning clinical labels or offering interpretations. This aligns with the receptive-style GIM tradition described in Grocke and Wigram (2007).

**Open question closure.** Music & Imaging turns end with open questions inviting the participant to notice further ("What do you sense in your body, or in the space around you?", "What do you notice as you can see a little farther ahead?"), without prescribing what to notice. This preserves the participant's own imagery process.

**Multi-dimensional state elicitation in Prelude.** The Prelude follow-up asks about heaviness across three dimensions ("emotionally, physically, or in your thoughts"), and about temporal patterns ("what seems to keep coming back"). This produces richer input for focus theme extraction and VA estimation than a single-question elicitation.

**Postlude integration synthesizes prior phases.** The Postlude summary recalls the Prelude focus theme ("heaviness in your chest and shoulders, pressure and tiredness, and the worry of deadlines and unfinished work") and integrates it with the Music & Imaging progression ("the path became moonlit, wider, softer, and more visible ahead"), producing a coherent closure of the session arc.

## Note on Deployed vs Released Wording

The GIMFlow prompts used in the studies (including this demonstration) include some therapist-oriented terminology inherited from the practical origins of GIM as a therapy method. The released prompts distributed with this repository use non-clinical wording aligned with the paper's framing. This distinction is disclosed in §8 of the paper and in Appendix A. The facilitator style patterns illustrated above are preserved in both versions; only the surface labels differ.
